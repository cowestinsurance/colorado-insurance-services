Colorado requires nearly all employers to carry workers compensation insurance — even if you have just one employee. Workers comp covers medical expenses, lost wages, and rehabilitation costs for employees who are injured or become ill because of their job. Failing to carry it can result in fines up to $500 per day and personal liability for all injury costs.

## Who Must Carry Workers Comp in Colorado?

Colorado law (C.R.S. § 8-40-301) is clear: **almost every employer with one or more employees must carry workers compensation insurance.** This includes:

- Full-time employees
- Part-time employees
- Seasonal and temporary workers
- Family members who work in the business

There are very few exceptions, and the penalties for non-compliance are severe.

## Exemptions

A small number of categories are exempt from Colorado's workers comp requirements:

- **Sole proprietors and partners** — You can opt out for yourself, but you must cover your employees
- **Certain corporate officers** — Officers who own at least 10% of the company can file an exemption
- **Independent contractors** — Truly independent contractors (not misclassified employees) don't need to be covered. Colorado scrutinizes this classification carefully.
- **Federal employees** — Covered under federal workers comp programs
- **Casual domestic workers** — Such as occasional babysitters

**Important:** Even if you're exempt, you can choose to opt in and purchase workers comp coverage for yourself. This is often smart — it protects your income if you're injured and can't work.

## Penalties for Non-Compliance

Colorado takes workers comp compliance seriously. If you're caught without coverage:

- **Fines of up to $500 per day** for each day you're uninsured
- **Stop work orders** — The state can shut down your business operations
- **Personal liability** — You become personally responsible for all medical costs, lost wages, and damages for any injured worker
- **Criminal penalties** — Willful failure to insure is a criminal offense
- **Loss of legal protections** — Workers comp provides employers with immunity from most lawsuits by injured employees. Without it, employees can sue you directly.

## How Workers Comp Premiums Are Calculated

Your workers comp premium is based on three main factors:

### 1. Classification Code
Every job type has a classification code assigned by the National Council on Compensation Insurance (NCCI). These codes reflect the risk level of the work. Office workers have low rates; roofers have high rates.

### 2. Payroll
Your premium is calculated as a rate per $100 of payroll. Higher payroll = higher premium. The formula is:

**Premium = (Payroll / 100) × Classification Rate × Experience Modification Rate**

### 3. Experience Modification Rate (EMR)
Your EMR compares your claims history to other businesses in your industry:

- **EMR of 1.0** = Average claims history (baseline)
- **EMR below 1.0** = Better than average (lower premium)
- **EMR above 1.0** = Worse than average (higher premium)

A new business typically starts with an EMR of 1.0 and it adjusts over time based on your actual claims experience.

## Average Costs in Colorado

Workers comp costs vary dramatically by industry. Some examples:

- **Office/clerical workers** — $0.15-0.40 per $100 of payroll
- **Retail** — $0.50-1.50 per $100 of payroll
- **Restaurants** — $1.00-3.00 per $100 of payroll
- **Construction (general)** — $3.00-10.00 per $100 of payroll
- **Roofing** — $15.00-30.00+ per $100 of payroll

For a small business with $200,000 in payroll and a moderate classification rate of $2.00 per $100, the annual premium would be approximately $4,000.

## How to Reduce Your Workers Comp Costs

- **Implement a safety program** — Documented workplace safety programs can qualify you for discounts and reduce claims
- **Return-to-work program** — Getting injured employees back to light duty quickly reduces claim costs
- **Accurate classification** — Make sure employees are classified correctly. If you have office staff classified as field workers, you're overpaying.
- **Pay-as-you-go billing** — Some carriers offer monthly billing based on actual payroll, avoiding large year-end audits
- **Shop multiple carriers** — As an independent agency, Colorado Insurance Services compares rates across carriers to find the best price for your risk profile
- **Manage your EMR** — Every claim affects your EMR for three years. Preventing claims is the best long-term cost reduction strategy.

## How to Get Workers Comp in Colorado

You have three options:

2. **State fund** — Pinnacol Assurance is Colorado's insurer of last resort for businesses that can't find coverage in the private market
3. **Self-insurance** — Large employers can apply to self-insure, but this requires state approval and significant financial reserves

## Filing a Workers Comp Claim

When an employee is injured on the job:

1. **Get medical attention immediately** — Employee health comes first
2. **Report the injury to your employer** within 4 days (employee's responsibility)
3. **Employer reports to the insurer** — You must report the injury to your workers comp carrier promptly
4. **File the First Report of Injury** — Required within 10 days of learning about the injury
5. **The carrier investigates and manages the claim** — Including medical treatment and wage replacement

## The Bottom Line

Workers comp isn't optional for most Colorado employers — it's the law. But beyond legal compliance, it protects both your employees and your business. Employees get medical care and wage replacement. You get protection from lawsuits and the financial risk of a workplace injury.
