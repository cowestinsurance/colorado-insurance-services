**Yes, uninsured motorist coverage is strongly recommended for Colorado drivers, and it may be one of the most important coverages on your auto insurance policy.** Approximately 16% of drivers in Colorado are uninsured — roughly one in every six cars on the road has no insurance at all. When you factor in drivers who carry only the bare minimum liability limits, the number of motorists who cannot fully cover the damages they cause is even higher.

Uninsured and underinsured motorist coverage (UM/UIM) protects you and your passengers when the at-fault driver cannot pay for the injuries and damages they cause. Without it, you could be left covering tens or even hundreds of thousands of dollars in medical bills and lost wages out of your own pocket.

## What Is Uninsured Motorist Coverage?

Uninsured motorist (UM) coverage pays for your injuries and, in some cases, your vehicle damage when you are hit by a driver who has no insurance at all. It essentially steps in and acts as if the at-fault driver had liability insurance — except the payment comes from your own policy.

UM coverage applies in several scenarios:

- **Hit by an uninsured driver:** The most straightforward scenario. The other driver is at fault and has no active insurance policy.
- **Hit-and-run accidents:** If the at-fault driver flees the scene and cannot be identified, your UM coverage can apply.
- **Pedestrian or cyclist accidents:** If you are hit by an uninsured driver while walking or cycling, your UM coverage from your auto policy may still protect you.

## What Is Underinsured Motorist Coverage?

Underinsured motorist (UIM) coverage is related but distinct. It applies when the at-fault driver does have insurance, but their coverage limits are not high enough to pay for all of your damages.

For example, if you suffer $100,000 in injuries and the at-fault driver carries only the Colorado minimum of $25,000 per person in bodily injury liability, their insurance pays $25,000 and your UIM coverage can pay up to its limit for the remaining $75,000.

In Colorado, UIM coverage pays the difference between what the at-fault driver's insurance covers and your actual damages, up to your UIM policy limit.

## How Colorado Law Handles UM/UIM

Colorado law requires insurance companies to offer uninsured and underinsured motorist coverage to every policyholder. However, it does not require you to purchase it. If you decline UM/UIM coverage, you must do so in writing.

This opt-out approach means that many Colorado drivers do have UM/UIM coverage, sometimes without even realizing it — it was included in their policy because they never actively rejected it. If you are unsure whether your policy includes UM/UIM, check your declarations page or call your agent.

### Key Points About Colorado UM/UIM Law

- Insurers must offer UM/UIM at limits equal to your liability limits.
- You can choose lower UM/UIM limits, but they cannot exceed your liability limits.
- You must sign a written rejection if you choose not to carry UM/UIM coverage.
- Colorado follows a "damages" approach to UIM, meaning your UIM coverage pays the gap between the at-fault driver's coverage and your actual damages.

## Why Colorado's Uninsured Driver Rate Matters

Colorado's uninsured motorist rate of approximately 16% is higher than the national average. This means that on any given stretch of I-25, C-470, or Highway 36 during your daily commute, a substantial number of the cars around you have no insurance whatsoever.

The reasons drivers go uninsured vary — cost, oversight, driving with a suspended license — but the result for you is the same: if one of them causes an accident, there is no insurance to compensate you for your injuries or vehicle damage.

Even among insured drivers, many carry only the state minimum of 25/50/15. If you are seriously injured by a driver with minimum coverage, that $25,000 per person limit will not come close to covering a hospital stay, surgery, physical therapy, and lost wages.

## UM vs. UIM: Understanding the Difference

While UM and UIM are often bundled together on a Colorado policy, they cover different situations:

| Feature | Uninsured Motorist (UM) | Underinsured Motorist (UIM) |
|---------|------------------------|----------------------------|
| **When it applies** | At-fault driver has NO insurance | At-fault driver has INSUFFICIENT insurance |
| **Hit-and-run coverage** | Yes | No (driver must be identified) |
| **How it pays** | Pays up to your UM limit | Pays the gap between other driver's coverage and your damages |
| **Most common scenarios** | Completely uninsured drivers, hit-and-runs | Drivers carrying state minimum or low limits |

Both coverages protect you from essentially the same risk — being injured by someone who cannot pay — but they trigger under different circumstances. Carrying both provides the most complete protection.

## Stacking UM/UIM Coverage in Colorado

Stacking is a concept that allows you to combine the UM/UIM limits from multiple vehicles on your policy for a higher total benefit.

### How Stacking Works

If you have two vehicles on your policy, each with $100,000 per person in UM/UIM coverage, stacking would allow you to access up to $200,000 in coverage for a single claim. Without stacking, you are limited to $100,000 regardless of how many vehicles are on your policy.

### Colorado's Position on Stacking

Colorado law generally allows stacking of UM/UIM coverages across multiple vehicles on the same policy, unless the policy specifically includes an anti-stacking provision. Many policies in Colorado do include anti-stacking language, so check your policy or ask your agent whether stacking applies to your coverage.

If stacking is available on your policy, it can provide a meaningful increase in protection without requiring you to purchase higher per-vehicle limits.

## How UM/UIM Works With Your Other Coverage

Understanding how UM/UIM interacts with your other coverages helps you see the complete picture of your protection.

### UM/UIM and Health Insurance

If you are injured by an uninsured driver, both your UM coverage and your health insurance may apply. Your health insurance can cover your medical bills, while your UM coverage can additionally compensate you for lost wages, pain and suffering, and other damages that health insurance does not cover.

### UM/UIM and MedPay

Medical payments coverage (MedPay) pays your medical bills regardless of fault and does not require you to prove the other driver was uninsured. UM/UIM and MedPay can work together — MedPay provides quick, no-fault payment for medical bills, while UM/UIM covers the broader range of damages when an uninsured or underinsured driver is at fault.

### UM/UIM and Collision Coverage

If your vehicle is damaged by an uninsured driver, your collision coverage will pay for vehicle repairs (minus your deductible). Uninsured motorist property damage (UMPD) coverage, if available on your policy, may also apply and can reimburse your deductible.

## How Much UM/UIM Coverage Should You Carry?

The right amount of UM/UIM coverage depends on your personal situation, but here are general guidelines:

- **At minimum, match your liability limits.** If you carry 100/300/100 in liability, carry 100/300 in UM/UIM. This ensures you are protecting yourself at the same level you are required to protect others.
- **Consider your health insurance.** If you have a high-deductible health plan or limited health coverage, higher UM/UIM limits provide an additional safety net.
- **Factor in your income.** Lost wages from a serious accident can be devastating. UM/UIM coverage helps replace income during your recovery.
- **Assess your risk exposure.** If you commute on busy Colorado highways, your statistical exposure to uninsured and underinsured drivers is higher.

## The Cost of UM/UIM Coverage

The good news is that UM/UIM coverage is relatively affordable compared to the protection it provides. Adding or increasing UM/UIM coverage typically costs $50 to $200 per year, depending on your limits and driving profile. For many Colorado drivers, this is some of the best value in their entire insurance portfolio.

## What Happens Without UM/UIM Coverage

If you are hit by an uninsured driver and you do not carry UM coverage, your options are limited:

- **Sue the at-fault driver:** You can file a lawsuit, but uninsured drivers often lack the assets to satisfy a judgment. You may win in court and still collect nothing.
- **Use your health insurance and collision coverage:** These can cover some costs, but they do not compensate you for lost wages, pain and suffering, or your collision deductible.
- **Pay out of pocket:** Without UM/UIM, the remaining costs fall on you.

None of these options are as effective as simply having UM/UIM coverage in place before an accident occurs.

With one in six Colorado drivers carrying no insurance at all, uninsured motorist coverage is not an optional add-on — it is a fundamental part of a sound auto insurance strategy. The relatively low cost of UM/UIM coverage makes it one of the most valuable protections available to Colorado drivers.
