A Certificate of Insurance (COI) is a one-page document that proves your business has insurance coverage. It summarizes your policy types, coverage limits, effective dates, and the name of your insurance company — without revealing the full details of your policy. COIs are one of the most commonly requested business documents in Colorado, and most businesses need to provide them regularly.

## What Information Is on a COI?

A standard Certificate of Insurance (using the ACORD 25 form) includes:

- **Certificate holder** — The person or company requesting the certificate
- **Named insured** — Your business name as it appears on the policy
- **Insurance company name and NAIC number**
- **Policy number and effective dates**
- **Types of coverage** — General liability, commercial auto, umbrella, workers comp, etc.
- **Coverage limits** — Per occurrence, aggregate, and any sub-limits
- **Description of operations** — What work the certificate relates to
- **Additional insured status** — Whether the certificate holder is added to your policy
- **Cancellation notice provisions**

## Who Needs a COI?

Almost every business will need to provide a COI at some point. Common situations include:

- **Signing a commercial lease** — Landlords require proof of liability and property coverage before you move in
- **Starting a contract or project** — General contractors require subcontractors to provide COIs before work begins
- **Bidding on jobs** — Many RFPs require a COI as part of the submission
- **Getting a business license** — Some Colorado municipalities require proof of insurance
- **Working with clients** — Enterprise clients often require vendors to show proof of coverage
- **Securing a business loan** — Lenders want to know their collateral is insured

## What Is an Additional Insured?

When someone asks to be named as an "additional insured" on your policy, they're requesting liability coverage under your policy for claims arising from your work. This is different from simply receiving a COI.

For example, if you're a subcontractor working on a building, the property owner may want to be listed as an additional insured on your general liability policy. If someone is injured by your work and sues both you and the property owner, your policy would cover both parties.

Adding an additional insured is done through an endorsement to your policy and may have a small additional cost.

## How to Get a COI

Getting a Certificate of Insurance is straightforward:

1. **Contact your insurance agent** — Call or email us with the details of who needs the certificate
2. **Provide the certificate holder's information** — Their legal name, address, and any specific requirements
3. **Specify any special requirements** — Additional insured status, specific coverage limits, or waiver of subrogation
4. **Receive your certificate** — Most agents can issue a COI the same day, often within hours

## Common COI Requirements by Industry

Different industries face different COI requirements:

- **Contractors and subcontractors** — GL, workers comp, commercial auto, and umbrella are typically required. General contractors often set minimum limits ($1M/$2M GL is standard).
- **Professional services** — Clients may require professional liability (E&O) in addition to GL.
- **Retail and restaurants** — Landlords require GL and commercial property. Some also require liquor liability.
- **IT and technology** — Clients increasingly require cyber liability coverage shown on the COI.

## Tips for Managing COIs

- **Keep a master list** of everyone who needs your COI and when they expire
- **Renew proactively** — When your policy renews, send updated COIs to all certificate holders before they ask
- **Understand the requirements before signing contracts** — Some contracts require coverage limits that may need a policy adjustment
- **Don't agree to insurance requirements you can't meet** — Talk to your agent first

## The Bottom Line

A COI is simply proof that you have insurance. It doesn't change your coverage or create new obligations — it just documents what coverage you already have. Having an agent who can issue certificates quickly and accurately makes your business operations smoother.
