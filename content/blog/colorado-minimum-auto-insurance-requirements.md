**Colorado requires all drivers to carry minimum auto insurance with liability limits of 25/50/15** — that is $25,000 per person for bodily injury, $50,000 per accident for bodily injury, and $15,000 per accident for property damage. While these minimums satisfy the legal requirement to drive in Colorado, they often fall far short of what you actually need to protect yourself financially in a serious accident.

This article explains exactly what Colorado's minimum requirements mean, what happens if you drive without insurance, and why most drivers should carry significantly more coverage.

## Understanding the 25/50/15 Minimums

Colorado's minimum liability requirements are expressed as three numbers: 25/50/15. Here is what each one means:

### $25,000 Per Person Bodily Injury Liability

This is the maximum your insurance will pay for injuries to any single person in an accident you cause. If one person's medical bills, lost wages, and pain and suffering exceed $25,000, you are personally responsible for the difference.

### $50,000 Per Accident Bodily Injury Liability

This is the total maximum your insurance will pay for all bodily injuries in a single accident you cause, regardless of how many people are hurt. If three people are injured and their combined costs reach $120,000, your insurance pays only $50,000, leaving you liable for the remaining $70,000.

### $15,000 Per Accident Property Damage Liability

This is the maximum your insurance will pay for damage you cause to other people's property — their vehicle, a fence, a building, or other structures. With the average new vehicle in Colorado costing well over $40,000, a $15,000 property damage limit can be exhausted very quickly.

## Why Minimum Coverage Is Often Not Enough

Colorado's minimum limits were established to provide a basic safety net, but they have not kept pace with the actual costs of accidents. Here is why minimums can leave you dangerously exposed:

### Medical Costs Have Skyrocketed

A single emergency room visit can easily exceed $25,000. A serious injury involving surgery, hospitalization, and rehabilitation can generate medical bills of $100,000 to $500,000 or more. The minimum $25,000 per person limit simply cannot cover a significant injury.

### Vehicles Are Expensive

The average price of a new car in Colorado exceeds $45,000, and many trucks and SUVs cost $60,000 or more. At the minimum $15,000 property damage limit, you could be personally liable for $30,000 or more just to replace the other driver's vehicle — not counting any damage to other property.

### You Are Personally Liable for the Gap

If the damages in an accident you cause exceed your policy limits, the injured party can sue you for the difference. This means your savings, wages, and other assets could be at risk. In Colorado, certain assets are protected from judgment, but the financial exposure is still significant.

### Multi-Vehicle Accidents Are Common

On Colorado's busy highways — I-25, I-70, and I-225 in particular — multi-vehicle accidents are not uncommon. When multiple vehicles and passengers are involved, the $50,000 per-accident limit is grossly inadequate.

## Recommended Coverage Levels for Colorado Drivers

Most insurance professionals recommend liability limits well above the state minimum. Common recommendations for Colorado drivers include:

- **50/100/50:** A reasonable step up that provides meaningfully better protection.
- **100/300/100:** A strong choice that covers most serious accident scenarios.
- **250/500/250 or higher:** Recommended for drivers with significant assets to protect.

The cost difference between minimum coverage and substantially higher limits is often surprisingly small — sometimes just $20 to $50 more per month for a dramatic increase in protection.

## Penalties for Driving Uninsured in Colorado

Colorado takes uninsured driving seriously. If you are caught driving without the required minimum insurance, you face significant consequences:

### First Offense

- Fine of up to $500
- Four points on your driving record
- Potential suspension of your vehicle registration
- Mandatory SR-22 filing requirement

### Subsequent Offenses

- Fines increase with each offense
- Longer registration suspension periods
- Extended SR-22 filing requirements
- Potential jail time in some circumstances

### If You Cause an Accident While Uninsured

- You are personally liable for all damages and injuries
- Your driver's license can be suspended
- You may face a civil lawsuit with no insurance company to defend you
- Judgment amounts can follow you for years

## SR-22 Requirements in Colorado

An SR-22 is not an insurance policy — it is a certificate filed by your insurance company with the Colorado DMV that proves you are carrying at least the minimum required coverage. You may be required to file an SR-22 if you:

- Were caught driving without insurance
- Had your license suspended for certain offenses
- Were convicted of DUI or DWAI
- Were involved in an at-fault accident while uninsured

### How the SR-22 Process Works

1. Contact an insurance company that offers SR-22 filings (not all do).
2. Purchase at least the minimum required auto insurance policy.
3. Ask your insurer to file the SR-22 form with the Colorado DMV on your behalf.
4. Maintain continuous coverage for the required period — typically three years in Colorado.
5. If your policy lapses for any reason during the SR-22 period, your insurer is required to notify the DMV, which can result in an immediate license suspension.

### SR-22 and Insurance Costs

Having an SR-22 requirement typically means higher insurance premiums, because the underlying reason for the SR-22 (DUI, lapse in coverage, etc.) places you in a higher risk category. Shopping with multiple carriers through an independent agent can help you find the most competitive rates even with an SR-22.

## Other Coverage Types Colorado Drivers Should Consider

Beyond liability, Colorado offers additional coverage types that provide important protection:

### Uninsured/Underinsured Motorist Coverage (UM/UIM)

With approximately 16% of Colorado drivers being uninsured, UM/UIM coverage protects you if you are hit by someone who has no insurance or inadequate coverage. Colorado requires insurers to offer this coverage, and you must actively reject it in writing if you do not want it.

### Medical Payments Coverage (MedPay)

MedPay covers medical expenses for you and your passengers after an accident, regardless of who is at fault. It fills gaps while other claims are being processed.

### Collision Coverage

This pays to repair or replace your vehicle after an accident, regardless of fault. It is typically required by lenders if you have a car loan or lease.

### Comprehensive Coverage

This covers non-collision damage to your vehicle — theft, vandalism, hail (a significant concern in Colorado), animal strikes, and natural disasters.

## How to Determine the Right Coverage Level

Choosing the right auto insurance coverage depends on your personal situation. Consider these factors:

- **Your assets:** The more you have to protect (home equity, savings, investments), the higher your liability limits should be.
- **Your income:** Higher earners face greater exposure because future wages can be garnished to satisfy a judgment.
- **Your vehicle:** Newer or more expensive vehicles warrant collision and comprehensive coverage.
- **Your risk tolerance:** Some drivers are comfortable with higher deductibles and moderate limits; others prefer maximum protection.
- **Your budget:** While more coverage costs more, the incremental cost of higher limits is often modest. Adjusting deductibles can help balance coverage and cost.

## The Bottom Line

Meeting Colorado's minimum auto insurance requirements keeps you legal, but it may not keep you financially safe. The 25/50/15 minimums can be quickly overwhelmed by the costs of a serious accident, leaving you personally responsible for the difference. Carrying higher liability limits, along with UM/UIM coverage, is a smart investment for any Colorado driver.
