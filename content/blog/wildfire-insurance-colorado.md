**Yes, standard home insurance policies in Colorado do cover wildfire damage.** Fire is one of the named perils included in virtually every homeowners insurance policy, meaning if a wildfire destroys or damages your home, your dwelling coverage, personal property coverage, and additional living expenses coverage should all apply. However, for homeowners in high-risk wildfire zones — and Colorado has many of them — obtaining and maintaining affordable coverage has become increasingly difficult in recent years.

This guide covers everything Colorado homeowners need to know about wildfire insurance, from understanding what is covered to navigating the challenges of insuring a home in a fire-prone area.

## Colorado's Growing Wildfire Risk

Colorado has experienced a dramatic increase in wildfire severity over the past two decades. The state's combination of dry climate, beetle-kill forests, expanding development into the wildland-urban interface (WUI), and increasingly warm temperatures has created conditions where devastating fires are no longer rare events.

### The Marshall Fire: A Wake-Up Call

The Marshall Fire in December 2021 was a turning point for wildfire awareness in Colorado. Driven by extreme winds, the fire tore through suburban communities in Boulder County, destroying over 1,000 homes and becoming the most destructive wildfire in Colorado history by property damage. What made the Marshall Fire especially significant was its location — this was not a remote mountain fire but a blaze that swept through established neighborhoods in Superior and Louisville.

The Marshall Fire demonstrated that wildfire risk in Colorado extends well beyond mountain communities. Grassland fires, wind-driven blazes, and fires in the WUI can threaten homes that many homeowners never considered to be at risk.

Other notable Colorado wildfires include the Cameron Peak Fire (2020), the East Troublesome Fire (2020), and the Waldo Canyon Fire (2012), each of which caused significant property damage and reshaped the insurance landscape in affected areas.

## What Standard Home Insurance Covers in a Wildfire

A standard homeowners policy provides several layers of protection related to wildfire:

### Dwelling Coverage

This pays to repair or rebuild your home if it is damaged or destroyed by wildfire. Your policy covers the structure up to your dwelling coverage limit, which should reflect the full cost of rebuilding your home at current construction prices.

### Other Structures

Detached garages, sheds, fences, decks, and other structures on your property are covered, typically up to 10% of your dwelling coverage.

### Personal Property

Your belongings inside the home — furniture, clothing, electronics, appliances — are covered up to your personal property limit. Creating a home inventory before disaster strikes is critical for maximizing this coverage.

### Additional Living Expenses (ALE)

If your home is uninhabitable due to wildfire damage, ALE coverage pays for temporary housing, meals, and other increased living costs while your home is being repaired or rebuilt. In Colorado, this coverage proved essential for Marshall Fire victims, many of whom were displaced for more than a year.

### Smoke Damage

Smoke damage is also covered under standard policies, even if the fire itself does not reach your property. Smoke can cause significant harm to interiors, HVAC systems, and personal belongings.

## The Challenge of Insuring High-Risk Properties

While wildfire is covered by standard home insurance, the challenge in Colorado is that many insurers are reluctant to write policies for homes in high-risk areas — or they charge significantly higher premiums to do so.

### Non-Renewals and Coverage Denials

Homeowners in mountain communities, foothill areas, and WUI zones across Colorado have increasingly faced non-renewal notices from their insurance companies. Insurers are using more sophisticated wildfire risk modeling tools, and properties that score high on these models may find their options limited.

If your insurer decides not to renew your policy, they must provide advance notice (typically 45 to 60 days in Colorado), giving you time to find alternative coverage.

### Higher Premiums in Fire-Prone Areas

Even when coverage is available, premiums for homes in high wildfire risk zones can be two to four times higher than comparable homes in lower-risk areas. This reflects the insurer's assessment of the probability and potential severity of wildfire losses.

### Limited Carrier Options

In the most fire-prone areas, homeowners may find that only a handful of carriers are willing to offer coverage. This reduced competition often means higher prices and fewer coverage options.

## Colorado's FAIR Plan

For homeowners who cannot find coverage in the standard insurance market, Colorado offers a **FAIR Plan** (Fair Access to Insurance Requirements). The FAIR Plan is an insurer of last resort — it provides basic fire and wildfire coverage when no other carrier will write a policy.

### What the FAIR Plan Covers

FAIR Plan policies typically provide basic dwelling and personal property coverage for fire-related perils. However, they generally do not include the comprehensive coverage of a standard homeowners policy. You may need a separate policy to cover perils like theft, liability, and water damage.

### Limitations to Be Aware Of

- Coverage limits may be lower than what you would get from a standard carrier.
- Premiums can be high, reflecting the elevated risk of the insured property.
- Coverage terms and conditions are more restrictive.
- The FAIR Plan should be viewed as a temporary solution while you work to improve your property's risk profile or find a standard market carrier.

## Defensible Space and Wildfire Mitigation

One of the most effective ways to both protect your home and improve your insurability is to create and maintain **defensible space** around your property. Many Colorado counties and fire districts have specific defensible space requirements.

### Zone 1: Immediate Zone (0-5 Feet)

- Use non-combustible landscaping materials directly adjacent to the home.
- Remove dead vegetation, leaves, and debris from the roof and gutters.
- Ensure no combustible materials are stored against the home.

### Zone 2: Intermediate Zone (5-30 Feet)

- Space trees so their canopies are at least 10 feet apart.
- Remove ladder fuels — vegetation that allows fire to climb from ground level into tree canopies.
- Keep grass mowed to a height of four inches or less.
- Store firewood at least 30 feet from structures.

### Zone 3: Extended Zone (30-100 Feet)

- Thin trees and remove dead wood.
- Create spacing between tree clusters.
- Remove heavy accumulations of ground debris.

### How Mitigation Helps With Insurance

Some insurers in Colorado offer premium discounts or are more willing to write policies for homes that have completed wildfire mitigation work. Documenting your defensible space and mitigation efforts — with photos and certification from local fire authorities when available — can strengthen your case when shopping for coverage.

Colorado programs like Firewise USA communities and local wildfire councils can help homeowners access resources, training, and sometimes funding for mitigation work.

## Steps to Take Before Wildfire Season

Preparing for wildfire is both a safety measure and an insurance measure. Consider these steps each year:

- **Review your policy limits.** Make sure your dwelling coverage reflects current rebuilding costs, which have increased significantly in recent years due to labor and material cost inflation.
- **Update your home inventory.** Document your belongings with photos, videos, and a written list. Store this documentation in the cloud or off-site.
- **Check your ALE coverage.** Understand how long your additional living expenses coverage lasts and what dollar limit applies. Rebuilding after a wildfire can take 12 to 24 months or longer.
- **Create and maintain defensible space.** Follow the zone guidelines above and document your work.
- **Prepare an evacuation plan.** Know your routes, have a go-bag ready, and understand your community's evacuation procedures.
- **Harden your home.** Consider ember-resistant vents, fire-rated roofing, tempered glass windows, and enclosed eaves. These improvements reduce wildfire risk and can positively impact your insurance options.

## Understanding Smoke Damage Claims

Even if a wildfire does not reach your property, smoke from nearby fires can cause substantial damage. Smoke can infiltrate your home through HVAC systems, open windows, and building gaps, leaving residue on walls, furniture, clothing, and electronics.

Smoke damage claims are covered under standard home insurance, but they require thorough documentation. If smoke from a nearby wildfire affects your home, document the odor, discoloration, and any health effects. Have your HVAC system inspected, and consider hiring a professional smoke remediation company for an assessment.

## After a Wildfire: Filing Your Claim

If your home is damaged or destroyed by wildfire, take these steps:

1. **Contact your insurer immediately** to report the loss and begin the claims process.
2. **Document everything** — photograph and video all damage before cleanup begins.
3. **Keep receipts** for all temporary living expenses, as these will be reimbursed under your ALE coverage.
4. **Get professional estimates** for repair or rebuilding costs.
5. **Be thorough with your personal property claim** — use your home inventory to ensure nothing is missed.
6. **Understand your policy's extended replacement cost provision**, if applicable, which can pay above your dwelling limit if rebuilding costs exceed your coverage amount.
