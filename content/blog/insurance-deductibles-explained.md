An insurance deductible is the amount you pay out of your own pocket before your insurance coverage kicks in. If you have a $1,000 deductible and file a $5,000 claim, you pay $1,000 and your insurance company pays the remaining $4,000. Deductibles exist to share risk between you and your insurer — and they directly affect how much you pay in premiums.

## How Deductibles Work

When you experience a covered loss, the claims process goes like this:

1. **You report the loss** to your insurance company
2. **An adjuster assesses the damage** and determines the total cost
3. **Your deductible is subtracted** from the claim amount
4. **Your insurer pays the remaining balance** up to your coverage limit

For example, a hailstorm damages your Colorado home's roof. The repair cost is $12,000 and your deductible is $2,500. You pay $2,500, and your insurance pays $9,500.

Your deductible applies **per claim**, not per year (unlike health insurance, which typically has an annual deductible).

## Types of Deductibles

### Flat Dollar Deductible
The most common type. A fixed amount — $500, $1,000, $2,500, etc. You know exactly what you'll pay out of pocket.

### Percentage Deductible
Calculated as a percentage of your home's insured value. Common for wind and hail coverage in Colorado.

**Example:** Your home is insured for $400,000 with a 2% hail deductible. Your deductible for a hail claim is $8,000 (2% of $400,000).

Percentage deductibles are very common in Colorado home insurance because of the high frequency of hail claims. Always check whether your policy has a separate percentage deductible for wind/hail — it can be significantly higher than your standard deductible.

### Split Deductible
Some policies have different deductibles for different types of claims. For example:
- $1,000 standard deductible for most claims
- 2% deductible for hail and wind claims
- $2,500 deductible for water damage claims

### Disappearing Deductible
Some carriers offer programs where your deductible decreases for each year you don't file a claim. After several claim-free years, your deductible may drop to $0.

## How Deductibles Affect Your Premium

There's a direct relationship between your deductible and your premium:

- **Higher deductible = Lower premium** — You're taking on more risk, so the insurer charges less
- **Lower deductible = Higher premium** — The insurer takes on more risk, so they charge more

The savings can be significant. Raising your home insurance deductible from $1,000 to $2,500 might save you 10-20% on your annual premium. Over several years without a claim, those savings can more than cover the higher deductible if you do eventually file.

## Choosing the Right Deductible

Consider these factors:

### Your Emergency Fund
Can you comfortably pay the deductible if you had to file a claim tomorrow? If a $5,000 deductible would create a financial hardship, it's too high — even if it lowers your premium.

### Your Claims History
If you rarely file claims, a higher deductible makes financial sense. You save on premiums every year and only pay the higher deductible on the rare occasion you have a claim.

### Your Risk Level
In Colorado, hail damage claims are common. If you live in a high-hail area, consider how your hail deductible interacts with the likelihood of filing a claim. A 2% deductible on a $500,000 home means $10,000 out of pocket for each hail claim.

### The Break-Even Math
Compare the annual premium savings against the difference in deductible amounts. If raising your deductible from $1,000 to $2,500 saves you $300/year, you'd break even in 5 years without a claim.

## Deductibles by Insurance Type

### Home Insurance
- Standard deductibles: $1,000-$5,000
- Hail/wind deductibles in Colorado: Often 1-2% of dwelling coverage
- Always check for separate percentage deductibles

### Auto Insurance
- Collision deductibles: $250-$1,000 (most common: $500)
- Comprehensive deductibles: $100-$1,000 (most common: $250-$500)
- Liability coverage has no deductible

### Business Insurance
- General liability: Usually $0-$1,000
- Commercial property: $1,000-$10,000+
- Workers comp: Typically no deductible for employees; some large employers have deductible programs

## Common Mistakes with Deductibles

- **Setting the deductible too high to save on premium** — Savings don't help if you can't afford the deductible when you need it
- **Not knowing about percentage deductibles** — Many Colorado homeowners are surprised by their hail deductible. Read your policy.
- **Filing small claims that barely exceed the deductible** — A $1,200 claim on a $1,000 deductible nets you only $200 but goes on your claims history, potentially raising future premiums
- **Forgetting deductibles apply per claim** — Two hail storms in one year means two deductibles

## The Bottom Line

Your deductible is one of the most important decisions you make when setting up your insurance. It affects both your premium and your out-of-pocket costs when you file a claim. The right deductible balances affordable premiums with a comfortable level of financial risk.
